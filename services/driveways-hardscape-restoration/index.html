Driveway Installation & Hardscape Repair in Little Rock, AR

New paver and concrete driveways, plus repair and releveling for hardscape that's already failing.

Overview

A driveway takes more consistent load than any other hardscape surface on your property — vehicle weight, daily traffic, and in central Arkansas, seasonal ground movement from expansive clay soil. Hardscape Pros installs paver and concrete driveways built for that load, and we also repair and relevel existing hardscape that's already heaving, sinking, or separating.

Driveway failures almost always trace back to the base, not the surface material. A paver or concrete driveway poured or set over an inadequate sub-base will crack, settle, or heave within a few years regardless of how good the pavers or concrete finish looks on day one. We excavate driveways deeper than patios, compact a thicker aggregate base rated for vehicle loads, and grade for positive drainage so water doesn't pool or undermine the base from beneath.

This page covers our five driveway and restoration services. Paver Driveway Installation and Hardscape Repair & Releveling are our two most-requested services, each with a dedicated page. Concrete Driveway Installation, Paver Sealing & Cleaning, and Retaining Wall Repair are covered in the sections below.

If your existing driveway, patio, or retaining wall is showing cracks, uneven pavers, or drainage problems, don't assume it needs a full replacement — a lot of hardscape damage can be repaired if the underlying base is still sound. We serve Little Rock, North Little Rock, Conway, Benton, Sherwood, and the surrounding metro, and every repair estimate includes an honest assessment of whether repair or replacement makes more sense for your situation.

Additional Driveways & Hardscape Restoration Services

Is a Concrete Driveway a Good Fit for Arkansas Clay Soil?

A concrete driveway can work well on Arkansas clay soil, but only when the sub-base and reinforcement account for how much that soil expands and contracts with moisture. Concrete is rigid, so unlike pavers, it can't flex with minor ground movement — which means the base preparation, control joint spacing, and slab thickness all matter more for concrete than for almost any other hardscape surface.

We pour driveways at a thickness rated for vehicle load, over a compacted aggregate base, with steel reinforcement and control joints spaced to manage cracking as the concrete cures and the soil beneath it shifts seasonally. We also grade the driveway to shed water toward the street or a drain rather than toward your garage or foundation, since standing water against a slab edge is one of the fastest ways to undermine a concrete driveway's base.

Concrete driveways cost less upfront than pavers in most cases and offer a clean, uniform surface, though repairs typically mean patching or replacing a full section rather than lifting and resetting individual units the way you can with pavers.

How Often Should You Seal a Paver Driveway or Patio?

Most paver driveways and patios should be cleaned and resealed roughly every two to three years, though high-traffic driveways exposed to vehicle oil and de-icing salt may need it sooner. Sealing doesn't just add shine — a quality sealer locks polymeric sand in the joints in place, reduces weed growth between pavers, and helps prevent oil, tire marks, and mildew from staining the paver surface.

Our sealing process starts with a pressure wash to strip existing sealer residue, dirt, and organic growth, followed by joint sand replacement where it's washed out or eroded, then a professional-grade sealer applied evenly across the surface. We use sealers suited to your paver type and finish preference, from a natural low-sheen look to a wet-look enhancer that deepens paver color.

Regular sealing is one of the most cost-effective things you can do to extend the life and appearance of an existing paver driveway or patio, especially compared to the cost of repairing or replacing pavers damaged by years of unsealed exposure.

How Do You Know If a Retaining Wall Needs Repair or Full Replacement?

A retaining wall generally needs repair rather than full replacement when the damage is isolated — a bulge in one section, a few displaced blocks, or a drainage problem that hasn't yet compromised the wall's overall structure. Full replacement is usually the right call when a wall is leaning along its entire length, has widespread cracking, or has been failing long enough that the soil behind it has already shifted significantly.

We assess wall condition by checking for consistent versus isolated bulging, testing whether drainage is functioning or blocked, and looking at how the wall's base has held up. Where the damage is isolated, we can often rebuild just the affected section, correct the drainage behind it, and tie the repair into the existing wall so it doesn't stand out.

Waiting on a failing retaining wall almost always makes the eventual fix more expensive, since continued water pressure and soil movement widen the area that needs to be rebuilt. If you're seeing early signs like a slight lean, cracking mortar, or water pooling at the base, it's worth getting it assessed before it becomes a full rebuild.

FAQ

Driveways & Hardscape Restoration Questions

Can you repair pavers without replacing the whole driveway?+

Yes, in most cases. We can lift the affected pavers, correct the base underneath, and reset the same pavers so the repair blends in, rather than replacing the entire driveway. We only recommend full replacement when the base failure is widespread.

How long does a paver driveway take to install?+

A typical residential paver driveway takes about one to two weeks from excavation to finished surface, depending on size, base depth required, and drainage work. We'll give you a written timeline at your free on-site estimate.

Do you offer financing for driveway or restoration projects?+

We don't offer in-house financing directly, but we're happy to provide a detailed written estimate you can use with your own financing option, and we break larger projects into clear, itemized pricing so you know exactly what you're paying for.
